SECTION 2

The Perceptron

Introducing the Perceptron

The perceptron is the simplest artificial neuron — the basic building block of all modern AI. Invented by Frank Rosenblatt in 1957–1958 at Cornell.

Biological Analogy

The formula is simple — just a weighted sum followed by a decision:

output = 1 if (w₁·x₁ + w₂·x₂ + w₃·x₃ + b) > 0 output = 0 otherwise Where: x = inputs, w = weights (learned), b = bias

The Perceptron as a Binary Classifier

A perceptron answers exactly one question: which of two categories does this belong to?

Examples: spam or not spam / cat or not cat. This is called binary classification.

The Core Limitation

A perceptron can only draw a straight line. What if the data cannot be separated by a straight line?

🎯 Key Insight to Land
This isn't a bug — it's a fundamental limitation of the architecture. A single layer of computation can only represent linear decision boundaries. Most real-world problems are NOT linearly separable. This is why we need more.
Analogy: Imagine trying to draw a single straight line through a city map to separate all the coffee shops from all the restaurants. You can't — they're scattered everywhere. You'd need a much more complex boundary.

SECTION 3

MLPs, Neural Networks, and Deep Learning

From One Neuron to Many

The fix is simple in concept: add more layers. Multiple perceptrons working together can draw multiple lines, and those combined lines can create any shape of boundary.

Architecture — Multilayer Perceptron

Layer TypeWhat It Does
Input LayerRaw data going in — pixels, numbers, text tokens. Not really a "computation" layer, just the entry point.
Hidden Layer(s)Intermediate layers that transform the data. These are the layers that "learn features." The key innovation.
Output LayerThe final prediction. Could be a category (0/1), a probability, a number, etc.
💡 Why "Hidden"?
When you look at a trained model from outside, you only see inputs and outputs. The hidden layers are doing work you can't directly observe. They're "hidden" in that sense — not secret, just internal.
📖 Terminology
A network with at least one hidden layer is called a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP). The term feedforward means information flows one direction only — input → hidden → output. No loops.

The Universal Approximation Theorem

Here's something remarkable. It's been mathematically proven that:

A neural network with just one hidden layer — if that layer is wide enough — can approximate any mathematical function to any degree of accuracy.

In plain terms: a large enough single-hidden-layer network can learn any pattern in any data. This is called the Universal Approximation Theorem (UAT).

So why don't we just use one huge hidden layer? Because "in theory" and "in practice" are very different. A single wide layer might need millions or billions of neurons for complex problems. It becomes extremely hard to train — the math gets unstable. It also overfits easily, memorising training data instead of learning the actual pattern. This is partly why AI research stalled in the 1980s–1990s: people knew neural networks could work; they couldn't make them actually work at scale.

Why Depth Changes Everything

The breakthrough: instead of one giant layer, use many smaller layers. This is deep learning — "deep" just means many layers.

Output layerFinal prediction
Hidden layer 4Detects full objects (faces, cars)
Hidden layer 3Detects parts (eyes, wheels)
Hidden layer 2Detects simple shapes (corners, curves)
Hidden layer 1Detects edges and gradients
Input layerRaw pixels

Each layer builds on what the previous layer learned. This hierarchical structure is enormously efficient — a 10-layer network of 100 neurons each dramatically outperforms a 1-layer network of 1000 neurons.

Benefits of depth over width:

PropertyWide + ShallowDeep + Narrow
Training difficultyVery hard — math gets unstableMuch easier to train
Parameter efficiencyNeeds billions of neuronsFar fewer total parameters
GeneralisationMemorises training dataLearns real patterns
Feature learningEverything at once — chaosHierarchical — simple → complex
Analogy: Compare writing a 10,000-word essay in one sitting versus in stages — outline first, then summaries, then rough draft, then final. The staged approach produces better results with less total effort. Deep networks work the same way.

SUMMARY

Recap & Key Concepts

End of class verbal review
QuestionAnswer
"What's the difference between AI and Machine Learning?" ML is a subset of AI — it learns patterns from data instead of following hand-coded rules
"What is a perceptron?" An artificial neuron — takes inputs, multiplies by weights, sums them, outputs 0 or 1
"Why can't a single perceptron solve XOR?" It can only draw one straight line — XOR is not linearly separable
"What's a hidden layer?" A layer of neurons between input and output that transforms the data into useful intermediate representations
"Why is deep better than wide?" More efficient, easier to train, learns hierarchical features — simpler patterns in early layers, complex in later layers
